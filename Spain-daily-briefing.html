The shape of the day

You land jet-lagged with hours to kill before the 3 PM room. The art of day one is staying upright until a normal bedtime — light activity, daylight, no long horizontal naps.

Airport → hotel (what to know)

  • Passport control can run 20–60+ min on a US arrival before you're landside. Build that into the morning.
  • To the hotel: taxi from T4 to Gran Vía is a fixed €33 flat rate (~25–35 min) — no meter games. Or Cercanías train C1 from inside T4 to central Madrid, then a short hop.
  • La Carmencita (8 PM, C/ Libertad in Chueca) is ~15 min walk from the Hyatt — your "second oldest restaurant," est. 1854.

Easy daytime to stay awake

  • Bus tour (your note): hop-on loop stops near Gran Vía / Plaza España — lowest-effort way to stay upright and oriented.
  • Retiro Park (20-min walk, flat) or the Book Fair along Paseo de Coches if it's still running.
Don't nap past ~3 PM or longer than 45 min. The 8 PM reservation is your anchor onto Spanish time.
Cards/cash: tell your bank you're in Spain (first foreign charge can auto-freeze). Visa works best; Amex is iffy. Take €100 notes or smaller from an ATM.

The shape of the day

Your only unscheduled full day in Madrid. Worth a loose plan so it doesn't evaporate. 8 PM at Botín is early by local standards (locals eat 9:30–10:30) — which is good, it leaves the evening open after.

Options — pick a lane

  • Art morning: the Golden Triangle — Prado, Thyssen, Reina Sofía (Guernica) — all within 10 min of each other. Pick one; don't try all three. Note: Reina Sofía is closed Tuesdays, so today the choice is Prado or Thyssen — save Guernica for June 3. Prado is free 6–8 PM but that clashes with Botín.
  • PHotoESPAÑA 2026: the photography festival is already running (it opened May 13) across CaixaForum, Círculo de Bellas Artes and other venues. Low-cost, atmospheric, very Madrid-in-June.
  • Royal core: Royal Palace + Almudena Cathedral + Plaza Mayor, then drift to La Latina for tapas. This also pre-walks tomorrow's Changing of the Guard ground.
  • Rooftop hour: Círculo de Bellas Artes azotea or the Riu rooftop at Plaza España for the late golden light before dinner.

Concerns & ins/outs

Botín books out. Arrive on time — they seat the famous cochinillo (suckling pig) and cordero (lamb) from the original wood oven. It's the signature; order it.
Pace it: tomorrow is your biggest Madrid day (guard + bullfight). Keep tonight from running too late.
Heat: June Madrid afternoons run hot and dry. Do museums/indoor 1–5 PM, save streets for morning and evening.

The shape of the day

Two ticketed/timed anchors at opposite ends of the day with a long free afternoon between. Morning = palace. Afternoon = rest/market. Evening = Las Ventas.

The afternoon = Reina Sofía window. Closed Tuesday (so not doable June 2), but open today — this is when to see Guernica. Go early/mid-afternoon as a paid visit; its free hours (Mon, Wed–Sat 7–9 PM) collide with the 7 PM bullfight, so don't bank on those. It sits at the south end of the Paseo del Prado (by Atocha), an easy Metro hop before heading to Las Ventas.

Changing of the Guard — ins/outs

  • The solemn ceremony (full band, cavalry) happens the first Wednesday monthly — June 3 qualifies. Far bigger than the weekly change.
  • Arrive by 11:30 for a spot along the Palacio Real / Plaza de la Armería railings. It runs ~30 min.
  • It's outdoors and sunny — hat, water.

Bullfight — the seating layout

  • Sombra (shade) is the premium side — sections 1, 2, 9, 10, plus Tendido Preferente (padded seats, most legroom).
  • Sol y Sombra (sections 3, 8) starts sunny, shade arrives partway through.
  • Sol (sun) sections 5–7 are full sun — but bearable at a 7 PM June start.
  • Lower rows (Tendido) are closest to the ring; Grada and Andanada climb higher.
Find your section number on the ticket and match it above so you know whether you're in sun or shade — and bring a hat if you're on the sol side.

Afternoon & after

  • Mercado de San Miguel (your note): touristy but fun for grazing — go off-peak (3–5 PM) to dodge crowds, or after the bullfight if you want late bites.
  • Las Ventas is on Line 2 (metro Ventas), ~15 min from center. Taxi back after dark is easy.
  • Optional late: Noches del Botánico concert series may have an opening night near this date.
Pack tonight. Tomorrow is a travel day to Sarria and the Camino begins. Your big bag gets transferred hotel-to-hotel on the walk — but you need walking kit accessible.

The shape of the day

Transition day. Morning in Madrid, ~4h train northwest into Galicia, evening orientation. Tonight you get your Orientation Packets, first Pilgrim's Passport stamp, and a Galician dinner (included).

The one that can't be missed

This is essentially the ONLY direct train of the day to Sarria. Unlike most Spanish routes, Madrid→Sarria runs about once daily. Miss the 13:20 and there's no clean same-day recovery — you'd be improvising a route via Ourense or Lugo. Treat this departure as the day's hard deadline.

Getting to Chamartín — ins/outs

  • Your ticket includes a FREE Cercanías ride to Chamartín. The "Combinado Cercanías" codes on your tickets (Margaret RU6F8 · Robert 5BGZY) cover the commuter train to the station, valid in the 4-hour window before departure. Don't pay €30 for a taxi you don't need — though with luggage, a taxi (~15–20 min, ~€30 flat) is still the low-stress choice.
  • It's Chamartín, NOT Atocha. Two different mainline stations across the city. Chamartín is to the north, ~15–20 min from Gran Vía.
  • Be at the platform 30+ min early. Renfe closes train access 2 minutes before departure — they mean it.
  • Journey ~4h 12m (13:20 → 17:32). The route may split/stop at Ourense; stay with the carriage marked for Sarria. This is the Alvia (loc RXF4WD) — not the AVLO you take home.
  • No food service on board ("Sin Restauración") — buy lunch at Chamartín before boarding.
17:32 arrival leaves a comfortable buffer before the 7:30 PM Fresco meet at Hotel Mar de Plata (Orientation Packets, first stamp, Galician dinner included).

Camino bag system (active from here)

One bag per person, max 20 kg / 44 lb, moves hotel-to-hotel by Fresco — strict on weight. Your daypack carries everything you need on the trail (water, rain shell, snacks, blister kit, passport, phone); you won't see the big bag until the next hotel each night.
Galicia rains unpredictably even in June — rain shell in the daypack every day, regardless of the morning sky.

Day notes

  • Depart Sarria following yellow arrows; Fresco's guide sends you off at Barbadelo, then you're solo on the Way.
  • Barbadelo (~4 km) has a Romanesque church and a popular café-bar — a natural first coffee stop. Around the midpoint, Casa Mirallos near Ferreiros is a well-known lunch spot.

Day notes

  • Dinner: for a meat-forward meal skip the pulperías and head to a grill — Parrillada/Raxería Ramos (Galician churrasco) or A Codeseira (beef ribs, pork ribs, picaña, roast lamb; allergens labeled). Several spots also do big salads and combo plates. Dinner service from ~8 PM.
Second 23 km day. By now you'll know your rhythm. Front-load the distance in the cooler morning hours.

The arrival

6 days, 115 km — you reach the cathedral. This is the emotional summit of the trip. Along the way you pass Lavacolla, where medieval pilgrims washed before entering the city, and climb Monte do Gozo ("Mount of Joy") — the first glimpse of the cathedral spires, ~4.5 km out.

What to do, in order

  • Pilgrims' Office (Rúa Carretas 33): final stamp + request your Compostela certificate. Lines build midday — go on arrival or early.
  • Pilgrims' Mass at the cathedral, noon (also evening masses). The botafumeiro (giant incense censer) swings on some days — not guaranteed.
  • Wander the medieval old town — Praza do Obradoiro, the cathedral facade, the buzz of arriving pilgrims.
Two passport stamps a day minimum over the last 100 km are what unlock the Compostela. If you've been collecting, you're set.
Hotel Compostela for the night of the 10th is your tour's final-night room (booked via Fresco). The post-tour nights (11–13) are separate reservations — see next days.

Today: Cape Finisterre

Your one fully open day in Santiago, and you're spending it on the Costa da Morte / Finisterre coast — the "End of the World" lighthouse the Romans believed marked the edge of the earth. You're booked on the standard full-day group tour.

The group tour

  • Full day, ~9 hours door to door. Escort with English audioguides. Pickup at Capilla del Pilar, Alameda Park.
  • Stops: Pontemaceira, the Muxía sanctuary, Cape Finisterre, the Ézaro waterfall, Carnota's long hórreo, and Muros.
It eats the whole day. ~9 hours door to door, and Finisterre is ~90 km each way — a real travel day, not a quick hop. Don't plan anything else for June 12.
Lunch isn't included — and heads up, Finisterre is a fishing port, so nearly every restaurant is seafood-first. Not ideal if you don't eat fish. The safe bet is Asador San Roque (at the town entrance) — a proper grill house known for char-grilled Galician beef, steaks, ribs and wood-fired pizza, with salads and non-fish options, while still doing seafood for anyone who wants it. Closed Mondays, but June 12 is a Friday, so you're fine.
